Alexander Jake Davies

Research

Alexander's doctoral thesis examines the impact of domestic politics on the credibility of alliance commitments. It does so by exploring the US-UK and US-France alliances over the course of the Cold War, using declassified archival material to assess how European leaders factored in the US' domestic politics to credibility calculations. Alexander's work departs from traditional accounts of credibility by arguing that domestic politics regulates the perceived 'usability' of power and that, in turn, this 'usable power' affects perceived credibility irregardless of a state's resolve and capabilities. In making this case, Alexander takes what he terms the 'inside-out' approach to assessing credibility, or in other words, exploring how credibility signals were received in practice as opposed to the intentions of the senders. This novel methodological approach provides a greater and more intimate understanding of how domestic politics impacts alliances. The thesis also points forward towards the contemporary era and explores the impact of fracturous contemporary politics on the US' ability to create sustainable and strong alliance networks, which provides a relevant and timely contribution to the academic literature.


Research Interests

 Outside of his doctoral thesis, Alexander explores credible commitments and domestic politics more broadly. He is able to provide expertise on US Politics, UK Politics, International Security, Alliances, Credibility, Grand Strategy, and Domestic Politics.
